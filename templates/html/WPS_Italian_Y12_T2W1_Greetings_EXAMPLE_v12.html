📋 Italian Lesson Plan — CRT Quick Reference

Westbourne Park School  |  Italian (LOTE)

Year 1/2
Term 2 Week 1 — 2026
P. Gallivan
VC2LOIT0101 – Communicating in Italian Year 1/2 40 min Classroom 22 students
📋 CRT Instructions — Lesson Overview
Focus / Topic
Greetings & Introductions in Italian
Resources
Greeting cards, puppets, Italian music, vocabulary poster
Room / Setup
Desks in a large circle; open floor space for mingle activity
Learning Intention
Greet people and introduce yourself in Italian
Learning Intention & Success Criteria
We are learning to greet people and introduce ourselves in Italian using key phrases.  I can: (1) say Ciao or Buongiorno to greet someone, (2) ask Come ti chiami? (What is your name?), (3) respond Mi chiamo [name] (My name is [name]).
Opening
① OPENING / ACTIVATION
5 min
Italian greeting song — build familiarity with key sounds, create a warm community atmosphere and hook into the language
1
Play "Ciao Bambini" greeting song (1:30 min). Students clap the beat and listen for familiar words. After: "Put your hand up if you heard a word you recognised."
2
Think-Pair-Share: "When do you say hello in English? Do different times of day have different greetings?" Discuss. "Italian has the same idea."
3
Show vocabulary poster: Ciao (casual), Buongiorno (good morning), Buonasera (good evening). "When do you think you'd use each one?"
📌 NOTE  📌 Some students may have Italian heritage and know these words already — invite them to be "language experts" during the lesson, not just learners.
→ "Now I'm going to show you how to have a full greeting conversation in Italian. Watch the puppets."
Explicit Teaching
② EXPLICIT TEACHING
8 min
Modelling a greeting exchange — use puppets to make the dialogue visual, repetitive and memorable
1
Puppet A: "Ciao!" Puppet B: "Ciao! Come ti chiami?" Puppet A: "Mi chiamo Lucia. E tu?" Puppet B: "Mi chiamo Marco. Piacere!" Perform twice, slowly.
2
Break it down: "Ciao = hi. Come ti chiami = what's your name? Mi chiamo [name] = my name is [name]. Piacere = nice to meet you." Point to vocab poster each time.
3
Whole class practice — teacher is Puppet A, students are Puppet B: "Buongiorno!" → "Buongiorno! Come ti chiami?" → "Mi chiamo [Teacher]. E tu?" → students say their own name.
❓ CFU  ❓ "Everyone: show me Ciao on your fingers." (1 finger) "Show me Come ti chiami." (2 fingers) "Show me Mi chiamo." (3 fingers) Quick signal check.
→ "Find a partner near you. You're going to practise this together."
We Do
③ WE DO (GUIDED)
10 min
Partner dialogue practice — students exchange the full greeting with a partner using vocab cards for support
1
Partner A greets Partner B: "Ciao! Come ti chiami?" Partner B responds: "Mi chiamo [name]. E tu?" Partner A responds with name. Both say "Piacere!"
2
New partner. This time: use Buongiorno instead of Ciao. Practise the full exchange again. Which greeting feels right for the morning?
3
Add a challenge: use the vocab card face-down. Can you remember the phrases without looking? Try once, then check.
📌 NOTE  📌 Students will giggle at pronunciation — this is GOOD. Celebrate the attempt. Correct gently with a model: "Great try — in Italian we say it like this: bwon-JORN-oh."
→ "Well done! You're going to greet 5 classmates now — starting positions, standing in a circle."
You Do
④ YOU DO (INDEPENDENT)
15 min
Mingle greeting activity — students independently move around the room greeting 5 different classmates using the full exchange
1
Mingle: "Walk around the room. When you meet someone, do the full Italian greeting — from Ciao to Piacere. Each time you complete a greeting, both tick a box on your record sheet."
2
After 3 minutes: "Switch — try to greet someone you haven't greeted yet. Try using Buongiorno with the next person instead of Ciao."
3
Challenge variation (last 2 min): greet a teacher, aide, or visiting adult in Italian. "Adults love it when kids speak Italian to them."
❓ CFU  ❓ "Hold up your record sheet — how many greetings? Did you remember all 4 phrases?" Quick show of record sheets.
→ "Back to your seats. Bring your record sheet."
Closure
⑤ CLOSURE & REFLECTION
7 min
Class debrief and reflection — consolidate vocabulary, celebrate effort, and connect language learning to cultural understanding
1
Return to seats. Collect record sheets.
2
Class vote: "Ciao or Buongiorno — which felt more natural? Which would you use with a friend? With an older person?" Cultural conversation.
3
Preview: "Next lesson we add numbers and counting — Un, due, tre! See you then." Exit: class says "Arrivederci!" (goodbye) in chorus.
📦 Resources & Materials
  • Italian greeting vocab poster (A3, displayed on board)
  • Italian puppet pair (or pictures of two characters)
  • Mingle record sheets (A5, one per student)
  • Vocabulary flip cards (one set per pair)
  • "Ciao Bambini" greeting song — Bluetooth speaker or tablet
⚠️ Safety & Wellbeing Notes
  • Mingle activity: no running; walking pace only in the classroom
  • Physical greeting (handshake) is optional — fist-bump alternative available
  • Inclusive language: affirm all pronunciation attempts warmly before modelling the correct version
  • Italian-heritage students: honour their knowledge, invite them to model, never put on the spot to perform if they choose not to
  • Stop signal: "Basta!" (enough/stop) with raised hand — establish and practise before mingle begins

Westbourne Park School  |  Italian (LOTE)  |  VTLM 2.0 Framework
Year 1/2
Term 2 Week 1 — 2026
P. Gallivan
Learning Intention
We are learning to greet people and introduce ourselves in Italian using key phrases: Ciao, Buongiorno, Come ti chiami?, Mi chiamo [name], Piacere.
Success Criteria
I can: (1) use Ciao or Buongiorno to greet someone appropriately, (2) ask Come ti chiami? to find out someone's name, (3) respond Mi chiamo [name] when asked for my name, (4) say Piacere to complete the greeting exchange.
Victorian Curriculum Links
VC2LOIT0101 — Greet and farewell people, introduce and ask about others. VC2LOIT0102 — Respond to questions and instructions in Italian in familiar classroom contexts.
Class Profile / Context
22 students (Y1/Y2 split). 4 EAL/D students. 2 students with Italian heritage (valuable resource). High energy, social learners — mingle activities suit this group well.
Evidence from Last Lesson
First lesson of new term. Students recall "Ciao" and counting to 5 from last term. Most cannot yet use a complete greeting exchange. Building on positive engagement from Term 1.
Victorian Curriculum 2.0 Achievement Standard — Foundation–Year 2 Italian
By the end of Year 2, students interact in Italian using familiar language for greetings, introductions and classroom routines. They respond to simple questions and instructions. They use key vocabulary including greetings, numbers, colours and classroom objects.

①ELEMENT 1 — PLANNING FOR LEARNING

VTLM 2.0
Prior Knowledge / Pre-Assessment
Students know "Ciao" from Term 1. Some know counting 1–5. No prior experience with question forms (Come ti chiami?) or responses (Mi chiamo). Italian-heritage students may know more.
Differentiation Planning
Tier 1 (Supported): Vocab card always visible. Reduce to: Ciao + Mi chiamo [name]. Partner guided by teacher aide. Core: Full 4-phrase exchange as planned. Tier 3 (Extension): Add: Quanti anni hai? (How old are you?) Ho [X] anni. (I am [X] years old.)
Links to Sequence / Unit Overview
Lesson 1 of 8 in "About Me" unit in Italian. Builds toward a simple self-introduction monologue in Lesson 6. Links to VC2LOIT0101.
Resources & Space Required
Desks pushed to walls, large circle space. Vocab poster on whiteboard. Puppets/pictures at front. Mingle record sheets distributed before activity.

②ELEMENT 2 — ENABLING LEARNING

VTLM 2.0
①
OPENING / ACTIVATION 5 minutes
Focus: Italian greeting song to activate prior learning, build cultural connection, and create a warm, social atmosphere before language instruction begins.
1
Play "Ciao Bambini" as students enter. Students sit in circle and clap the beat. First listen — just enjoy. "What Italian words did you hear?"
2
Think-Pair-Share: "How do YOU say hello? Do you say different things at different times of day?" Draw out: morning, evening, formal, casual.
3
Introduce vocab poster: Ciao (casual, any time), Buongiorno (formal, morning), Buonasera (formal, evening). Class says each 3× with the poster.
📌 Teacher Note
Italian-heritage students: ask them first — "Do you know this word? How does your family say it?" This builds cultural respect and positions them as language models.
→ Transition: "Watch the puppets — they're going to show us a whole conversation in Italian."

③ELEMENT 3 — EXPLICIT TEACHING

VTLM 2.0
②
EXPLICIT TEACHING — I DO 8 minutes
Focus: Puppet dialogue modelling a complete greeting exchange — repetitive and visual to support early language acquisition.
1
First viewing: Puppet dialogue (full speed): Ciao → Come ti chiami? → Mi chiamo Lucia → E tu? → Mi chiamo Marco → Piacere! "What did you notice? What's happening in the conversation?"
2
Break it down: Replay slowly. Stop at each phrase: "Ciao = hi. Come ti chiami = what is your name. Mi chiamo [name] = my name is [name]. Piacere = nice to meet you." Point to poster. Class repeats each.
3
Teacher ↔ class dialogue: Teacher as Puppet A: "Buongiorno! Come ti chiami?" Whole class responds: "Mi chiamo [each student says their own name]." Then: "E tu?" Teacher: "Mi chiamo [Teacher name]. Piacere!" Class: "Piacere!"
4
Pair practice 1: Students sit with the partner next to them in the circle. Take turns — A greets B, B greets A. Vocab card face-up. Teacher and aide circulate.
📌 Teaching Notes
Accept and celebrate all pronunciation attempts. Never say "wrong" — say "beautiful try — the Italian way sounds like this:" and model warmly. Buongiorno is often mispronounced: bwon-JORN-oh (silent g). Piacere: pyah-CHEH-reh.
❓ Check for Understanding
"Show me 1 finger for Ciao, 2 fingers for Come ti chiami, 3 fingers for Mi chiamo." Call them out: "Come ti chiami?" — students hold up 2 fingers. Quick whole-class scan.
→ Transition: "Excellent Italian! Now stand up — we're going to use these phrases with EVERYONE in the room."

④ELEMENT 4 — SUPPORTED APPLICATION

VTLM 2.0
③
WE DO — GUIDED PRACTICE 10 minutes
Focus: Guided partner practice with vocab support — students develop confidence through repeated, low-stakes paired exchanges before the independent mingle.
1
Partner A reads their greeting from vocab card, Partner B responds. Full exchange: Ciao/Buongiorno → Come ti chiami? → Mi chiamo [name]. E tu? → Mi chiamo [name]. Piacere!
2
New partner (move one seat clockwise). This time: Partner A uses Buongiorno instead of Ciao. Both try the exchange without looking at card.
3
Speed round: same partner again, go as fast as you can while still being correct. Who can do the full exchange in under 15 seconds?
❓ Checking for Understanding
"Thumbs up if you can do the greeting without looking at your card. Thumbs sideways if you still need it. No thumbs down — we're all learning." Adjust support accordingly.
→ Transition: "Distribute record sheets. Find a space to stand. When I say 'Via!' (go!) start greeting — five different people, five ticks."
📊 Differentiated Station Tasks — Element 4 Application
🔴 Tier 1 — Supported 🔵 Core Task 🟢 Tier 3 — Extension
Vocab card always visible. Greeting reduced to: Ciao + Mi chiamo [name]. Partner paired with teacher aide for first 2 exchanges. Full 4-phrase exchange: Ciao/Buongiorno, Come ti chiami?, Mi chiamo [name], Piacere. Vocab card available but encouraged to try from memory. Add: Quanti anni hai? (How old are you?) Ho [X] anni. (I am [X] years old.) Teach this to 2 other students during mingle.
Supported: 3 exchanges with card visible. Aide scribes ticks. Focus on Ciao + Mi chiamo [name] only. Celebrate every attempt. Core: 5 exchanges with full 4-phrase dialogue. Record sheet completed. Try at least 2 exchanges from memory. Extension: Complete 5 exchanges + teach Quanti anni hai? to 2 students. Record which students now know the extension phrase.
④
YOU DO — INDEPENDENT APPLICATION 15 minutes
Focus: Mingle greeting activity — students independently greet 5 classmates using the complete Italian exchange; teacher assesses fluency and confidence.
1
Mingle Round 1: Move around room. Greet any classmate: full exchange from Ciao to Piacere. Both partners tick their record sheet. Aim for 5 ticks.
2
Mingle Round 2: Switch to Buongiorno for the next 2 greetings. "Can you use the formal greeting correctly?" Record which greeting you used.
3
Challenge: Greet a teacher, aide, or visitor in Italian. They will respond. "Adults love hearing Italian!"
📌 Assessment Observation Window
Assessing VC2LOIT0101 — greeting exchange. Target 6 students — observe during mingle for: correct phrase sequence, appropriate greeting word choice (Ciao vs Buongiorno), pronunciation confidence. Tick/cross/note on clipboard.
→ Transition: "Basta! (Freeze) — return to your seats and bring your record sheets."

⑤ELEMENT 5 — REFLECTION ON LEARNING

VTLM 2.0
⑤
PACK UP & STUDENT REFLECTION 7 minutes
1
Students return to circle. Record sheets collected.
2
Vocabulary review: "I say the Italian, you say the English — Ciao!... Buongiorno!... Come ti chiami?... Mi chiamo..." Quick chorus.
3
Cultural reflection: "Why do Italians have different greetings for different times of day? How is that the same or different from your family?" Exit: everyone says "Arrivederci!" together.
❓ Exit Reflection
How many students could complete the exchange independently (without card)? Who still needs the card? Were pronunciation attempts improving? Who knows the extension phrases?
💚 Wellbeing & Social-Emotional Learning (SEL)
Wellbeing Focus
Language learning requires risk-taking. Celebrate wrong attempts: "Bravissimo! — excellent try. That's exactly the kind of risk that grows your brain." Create a classroom norm: in Italian, mistakes are welcome.
Social Skill Target
Mingle activity builds community connection across year levels (Y1 and Y2). Students learn each other's names in Italian context. For shy students: start with a known friend, gradually move to less familiar peers.
Emotional Regulation
Mingle energy can escalate — establish and practise the "Basta!" stop signal before starting. Students with sensory needs can observe mingle from the side and complete a written version instead.
♿ Inclusive Practice & Adjustments
EAL/D Students
EAL/D students are learning BOTH English and Italian simultaneously — honour this. Comparisons to home language are welcome: "How do you say hello in Vietnamese? How is that similar to Ciao?" Reduces cognitive load and validates home language.
Learning / Physical Support
Students with learning support: reduce exchange to 2 phrases (Ciao + Mi chiamo). Use picture cards as prompts. Aide supports during mingle. Celebrate every attempt equally.
Extension / Gifted
Italian-heritage students: extra phrases (Quanti anni hai?). Role as "language expert" — peers can ask them for help. This builds identity connection and pride.
Medical / Withdrawal Notes
One student with selective mutism — offer alternative: point to phrase on card instead of speaking. Written greeting card exchange instead of verbal. Do not force speaking.
📊 Assessment & Evidence
Assessment Strategy
Observation during mingle — clipboard with class list. Look for: correct phrase sequence, Ciao vs Buongiorno distinction, pronunciation confidence. Exit chorus as whole-class check.
