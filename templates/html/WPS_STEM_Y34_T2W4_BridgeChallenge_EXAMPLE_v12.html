📋 STEM / Design Lesson Plan — CRT Quick Reference

Westbourne Park School  |  STEM / Design

Year 3/4
Term 2 Week 4 — 2026
P. Gallivan
VC2TDE2301 – Technologies Design Year 3/4 60 min Classroom / STEM Room 24 students
📋 CRT Instructions — Lesson Overview
Focus / Topic
Engineering Challenge — Build the Strongest Bridge
Resources
Index cards, tape, scissors, paper clips, weights (coins)
Room / Setup
Groups of 4 at tables; 2 stacks of books as bridge supports at each table
Learning Intention
Design, build, test, improve — engineering process
Learning Intention & Success Criteria
We are learning to use the engineering design process (plan → build → test → improve) to design and construct a bridge that holds maximum weight.  I can: (1) draw and label a design plan before building, (2) build a bridge using only the provided materials, (3) test my bridge by adding weight and record the result, (4) identify one change to improve my bridge and explain why.
Opening
① OPENING / ACTIVATION
5 min
Launch challenge — activate prior knowledge about structures and generate excitement through a real-world hook
1
Show 3 images of real bridges on the board (simple beam, arch, suspension). "How do you think engineers decided WHAT to build? Do you think they just started building?" Discussion.
2
Quick demo: place one index card flat between two stacks of books. Place a coin on it — it collapses. "So how do engineers make structures STRONGER?" Students brainstorm.
3
Show a folded accordion card. Place the coin — it holds. "Engineers use SHAPE and STRUCTURE to distribute weight. Today you're engineers."
📌 NOTE  📌 Let students struggle with the initial demo question before showing the accordion answer. The "aha" moment is pedagogically powerful — don't shortcut it.
→ "Before we build, we're going to learn the process engineers use. Gather on the mat."
Explicit Teaching
② EXPLICIT TEACHING
8 min
Explicit teaching of the engineering design process: Plan → Build → Test → Improve, with emphasis on drawing a labelled design before touching materials
1
Write on board: PLAN → BUILD → TEST → IMPROVE. "Engineers NEVER build first. Step 1 is always a plan." Show a simple labelled bridge design on board.
2
Think-aloud: "In my plan I need to label: what materials I'm using, how long my bridge is, and what structural feature I'm using to hold weight." Model labelling.
3
"Your bridge must: span a 20cm gap (the two book stacks), support as much weight as possible, be built ONLY with the materials on your table." Show material tray.
❓ CFU  ❓ "Before I give materials, everyone draws a labelled plan. Hold up your plan — I need to see it before materials are released." Scan for labels and a bridge shape.
→ "Engineers — move to your group. You have 4 minutes to agree on a design and draw your group plan. Materials released when I see your plan."
We Do
③ WE DO (GUIDED)
10 min
Guided building with teacher checkpoints — groups build their designed bridge with teacher circulating and prompting engineering thinking
1
Groups begin building following their plan. Teacher circulates: "Is your bridge matching your plan? What did you need to change and why?"
2
Mid-build checkpoint: "Stop. Look at your bridge. Predict — will it hold 5 coins? 10? What's your confidence level?" Groups record their prediction.
3
Groups continue. Teacher prompts: "What structural technique are you using? How does folding/rolling/layering distribute the weight?"
📌 NOTE  📌 Watch for groups that abandon their plan immediately. Redirect: "Engineers iterate on their design, they don't start from scratch unless they TEST first."
→ "Building time ends in 2 minutes. Prepare your bridge for testing — coins to the centre of the table."
You Do
④ YOU DO (INDEPENDENT)
15 min
Independent testing and improvement phase — groups test their bridges, record data, identify improvements, and rebuild if time allows
1
Testing: add coins one at a time to the centre of the bridge. Count aloud. Record maximum coins held before collapse in data table.
2
Analyse: "What caused the collapse? Where did the failure happen?" Identify one structural improvement. Draw the "v2 plan" — what would you change?
3
If time: rebuild with the one improvement and re-test. Compare results. Which structural feature made the biggest difference?
❓ CFU  ❓ "Fill in: Our bridge held ___ coins. We would improve it by ___ because ___." Quick written response on data sheet.
→ "Record your final result on the class data chart on the board. Clean up materials into the tray."
Closure
⑤ CLOSURE & REFLECTION
7 min
Class debrief — compare results, identify winning structural features, connect to real engineering principles
1
Clean up: materials to tray, tables clear. Display bridges or results chart.
2
Class data: "Which bridge held the most weight? What did the top 3 bridges have in common?" Students identify patterns (folded card, triangular supports, layering).
3
Connect to LI: "You just used the engineering design process. Which step do you think is most important? Why?" Preview: next lesson explores bridge design through history.
📦 Resources & Materials
  • Index cards (10 per group)
  • Masking tape (30cm per group)
  • Scissors (1 pair per student)
  • Paper clips (6 per group)
  • Coins for weight testing (20 per group) + class data chart (whiteboard)
⚠️ Safety & Wellbeing Notes
  • Scissors: cut away from body, walk with point down
  • Tape: not for use on other students
  • Collapse is expected — coins will fall. All students stand back during testing.
  • No throwing materials — any student who does is removed from the building phase
  • Clean up promptly — scissors and coins present small hazards on the floor

Learning Intention
We are learning to use the engineering design process — plan, build, test, improve — to design and construct a bridge that holds maximum weight using limited materials.
Success Criteria
I can: (1) draw and label a design plan before touching materials, (2) build a bridge that spans 20cm using only provided materials, (3) test, record, and explain my bridge's performance, (4) identify and implement one structural improvement.
Victorian Curriculum Links
VC2TDE2301 — Identify the design process and apply it to create a solution. VC2TDE2302 — Investigate the properties of materials and how they affect design outcomes.
Class Profile / Context
24 students. 3 EAL/D. High energy group — benefit from structured challenge with clear constraints. Two students with IEPs (reading/writing) — verbal recording acceptable.
Evidence from Last Lesson
Last lesson: students explored properties of card — rolling, folding, layering. Most understand that shape affects strength. Ready for full design-build-test cycle today.
Victorian Curriculum 2.0 Achievement Standard — Year 3/4 Technologies Design
By the end of Year 4, students describe how people use design processes to create designed solutions. They use the design process and select materials purposefully to create solutions, and evaluate their solutions against their design criteria.

①ELEMENT 1 — PLANNING FOR LEARNING

VTLM 2.0
Prior Knowledge / Pre-Assessment
Students have explored material properties (lesson 1). They know that shape affects strength (accordion fold). They have not completed a full design-build-test cycle yet.
Differentiation Planning
Tier 1 (Supported): Pre-drawn design template (outline of bridge to fill in). Reduce gap to 15cm. Partner with strong builder. Verbal recording of data. Core: Full design-build-test-improve cycle as planned. Tier 3 (Extension): Must use exactly 5 cards (constraint design). Write an engineering evaluation including force language (load, compression, tension). Research a real bridge type that uses their structure.
Links to Sequence / Unit Overview
Lesson 4 of 6 in "Engineering and Structures" unit. Builds toward a design brief for a school garden bridge in Lesson 6. Links to VC2TDE2301.
Resources & Space Required
Group tables with 2 stacks of books per table (20cm gap marked with tape). Material trays NOT distributed until plans are approved. Class data chart drawn on whiteboard.

②ELEMENT 2 — ENABLING LEARNING

VTLM 2.0
①
OPENING / ACTIVATION 5 minutes
Focus: Real-world hook to activate prior knowledge about structures and generate engineering curiosity before introducing the design process.
1
Show 3 bridge images (beam, arch, suspension). "What do you notice about how each one holds weight? Where does the force go?" 2–3 responses.
2
Demo: flat index card collapses under one coin. "So what makes a structure strong enough to hold weight?" Students brainstorm — no wrong answers.
3
Demo: accordion-folded card holds multiple coins. "Engineers use shape to distribute force — the same amount of material, completely different strength."
📌 Teacher Note
Resist explaining the accordion immediately — the power is in students working it out. Ask: "What's different between these two cards?" before showing the fold.
→ Transition: "Sit on the mat — I'm going to show you the process that every engineer follows before they pick up a tool."

③ELEMENT 3 — EXPLICIT TEACHING

VTLM 2.0
②
EXPLICIT TEACHING — I DO 8 minutes
Focus: Explicit teaching of the 4-step engineering design process with emphasis on planning before building.
1
Activate prior knowledge: "What do you think engineers do before they build a bridge? Do they just grab materials?" Draw out: they plan. They draw. They calculate.
2
Introduce the process: Write PLAN → BUILD → TEST → IMPROVE on board. "Skipping Plan means wasting materials, time, and money. Real engineers NEVER start without a plan." Model drawing a labelled bridge plan.
3
Challenge constraints: "Your bridge must span 20cm exactly. Use ONLY: 10 index cards, 30cm tape, 6 paper clips. No other materials." Write constraints on board. "Why do engineers work with constraints?"
4
Plan checkpoint: "Before materials are released — draw your group plan. Label your materials, your structural feature, and your predicted strength." Demonstrate a complete labelled plan on the board.
📌 Teaching Notes
Students often want to start immediately. Holding the plan requirement firm is part of the lesson. Walk around during planning and redirect: "What structural technique from last lesson are you using? Have you labelled it?"
❓ Check for Understanding
"Hold up your individual plan before your group plan begins." Quick scan: Does it show a bridge shape? Does it have labels? Approve or prompt for more detail.
→ Transition: "Move to your groups. You have 4 minutes for your group plan. Materials released when I approve your plan — I'm coming around now."

④ELEMENT 4 — SUPPORTED APPLICATION

VTLM 2.0
③
WE DO — GUIDED PRACTICE 10 minutes
Focus: Guided building with teacher circulating to prompt engineering reasoning — not troubleshooting for students but asking questions that develop their own problem-solving.
1
Groups build following plan. Teacher circulates: "Is this matching your plan? What did you need to change? WHY did you change it — that's the important question."
2
Mid-build pause: "Hands up — what structural technique is your group using?" Groups share. Connect to real bridge types on board image.
3
Second circulate: "Predict your maximum weight. Write it down before you test. Prediction is part of science — right or wrong, it's useful data."
❓ Checking for Understanding
"Group by group: what is your main structural feature? How does it help distribute weight?" 30-second per group. Look for understanding of shape-strength relationship.
→ Transition: "Building closes in 90 seconds. Prepare your bridge and your data sheet. Coins in the centre of the table."
📊 Differentiated Station Tasks — Element 4 Application
🔴 Tier 1 — Supported 🔵 Core Task 🟢 Tier 3 — Extension
Pre-drawn design template. 15cm span. Verbal data recording (teacher aide scribes). Focus on build and test phases — skip the improve iteration if time is short. Full design-build-test-improve cycle with 20cm span. Complete data table. Identify one improvement and explain why. Constraint design: exactly 5 cards. Written engineering evaluation using load/compression/tension vocabulary. Research connection to a real bridge type.
Supported: Use design template with outlines to fill in. 15cm gap. Partner with strong designer. Focus on understanding test results verbally. Core: Full cycle — plan, build, test, record results, identify and implement one improvement, compare before/after. Extension: 5-card constraint. Engineering evaluation paragraph with force vocabulary. Identify and research the real-world bridge type that matches their structure.
④
YOU DO — INDEPENDENT APPLICATION 15 minutes
Focus: Test, record, improve cycle — students work independently through testing and analysis while teacher circulates and records assessment observations.
1
Test: Add coins one at a time. Count and record. "What happened just before it collapsed? Where did it fail?" Record in data table.
2
Analyse: "What caused the failure? Was it the material? The structure? The join?" Identify ONE specific improvement to make.
3
Improve (if time): Draw v2 plan. Rebuild with the one change. Re-test. Compare: did the improvement work? Record the new result.
📌 Assessment Observation Window
Assessing VC2TDE2301 — design process. Look for: labelled plan present, purposeful material use, verbal explanation of structural choice. Target 8 students with clipboard. Photograph designs for portfolio.
→ Transition: "Record your final score on the class chart on the board. Materials to tray. Bridges stay on the desk for display."

⑤ELEMENT 5 — REFLECTION ON LEARNING

VTLM 2.0
⑤
PACK UP & STUDENT REFLECTION 7 minutes
1
Materials back to tray. Scissors to the front. Data sheets complete.
2
Class data debrief: "Top 3 bridge scores — what did they have in common? What structural feature appeared in the strongest bridges?"
3
Connect to LI: "Which step of the design process mattered most today? Why?" Preview: next lesson we look at bridge design through history and see if our solutions match what engineers discovered centuries ago.
❓ Exit Reflection
What proportion followed the plan before building? Which structural features dominated the top scores? Who needs support understanding the test/improve cycle next lesson?
💚 Wellbeing & Social-Emotional Learning (SEL)
Wellbeing Focus
Engineering normalises failure — the bridge collapsing is DATA, not failure. Model language: "Our bridge collapsed at 8 coins — that's useful information. Now we can improve it."
Social Skill Target
Group work: each member must contribute to the plan drawing. Assign roles (designer, builder, tester, recorder) to ensure equitable participation and prevent one student dominating.
Emotional Regulation
Competitive energy can escalate. Reframe: "The goal is to improve YOUR bridge from test to test, not just beat other groups." Celebrate design thinking, not just the highest score.
♿ Inclusive Practice & Adjustments
EAL/D Students
EAL/D students: visual design templates, physical demonstration of constraints, gesture-based instructions. Pair with bilingual buddy if available.
Learning / Physical Support
2 students with reading/writing IEPs: verbal data recording with teacher aide scribing. Pre-drawn design template. Reduce written expectations — focus on the build/test experience.
Extension / Gifted
Constraint design (5 cards) and engineering evaluation paragraph. These students identified by circulating teacher — offer the extension mid-build.
Medical / Withdrawal Notes
No specific medical notes for this class. General supervision during scissors use. Coins present a swallowing risk — ensure no students put materials in mouth.
📊 Assessment & Evidence
Assessment Strategy
Observation during design and build — focus on design process adherence. Data tables as work samples. Photograph labelled plans. Oral explanation during circulate counts toward assessment.
